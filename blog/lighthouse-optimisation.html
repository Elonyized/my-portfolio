From 42 to 96: A Lighthouse Score Rescue Story

May 12, 2026 6 min read Sule ThankGod

A client came to me with a Lighthouse performance score of 42. Pages took over six seconds to become interactive on mobile, and their checkout conversion rate had been quietly declining for months. This is the story of the five changes that moved the score to 96 — and lifted checkout conversion by 22% along the way.

Where the audit started

MetricBeforeAfter
Performance score4296
Largest Contentful Paint5.8s1.4s
Total Blocking Time1,240ms90ms
Cumulative Layout Shift0.310.02

1. Images were the single biggest offender

The product pages were serving full-resolution PNGs — some over 4MB — and letting the browser scale them down with CSS. Converting everything to responsive WebP with proper srcset attributes alone took LCP from 5.8s to 3.1s.

<img
  src="product-800.webp"
  srcset="product-400.webp 400w, product-800.webp 800w, product-1200.webp 1200w"
  sizes="(max-width: 600px) 100vw, 50vw"
  loading="lazy"
  alt="Product name"
/>

Note that the hero image — the one above the fold — was deliberately excluded from loading="lazy". Lazy-loading your LCP element delays it further and actively hurts the score.

2. Render-blocking scripts were loaded synchronously

Three third-party scripts — a chat widget, an analytics tag, and an old A/B testing library nobody remembered installing — were all loaded in the <head> without async or defer. Each one blocked the main thread before first paint.

<script src="chat-widget.js" defer></script>
<script src="analytics.js" async></script>

The unused A/B testing library was simply removed. That single deletion cut Total Blocking Time by roughly a third.

3. Layout shift was coming from unsized elements

A CLS of 0.31 meant the page was visibly jumping as it loaded — images and ads pushed content down after the user had already started reading. The fix was almost entirely mechanical: every <img> and embed got explicit width and height attributes, and reserved space was added for the cookie banner so it stopped pushing the hero down by 80px after the page settled.

4. Fonts were causing a flash of invisible text

The site loaded four font weights from Google Fonts with the default font-display behaviour, which hides text until the font arrives. Switching to font-display: swap and trimming to two weights removed nearly 400ms of invisible-text time.

5. The server itself was slow to respond

Time to First Byte was sitting at 800ms on a shared hosting plan with no caching layer. Moving static assets behind a CDN and adding server-side page caching brought TTFB down to 110ms — a change that had nothing to do with front-end code but mattered more than most of the front-end fixes combined.

Key takeaways

  • Audit images first — they're usually the largest, easiest win.
  • Never lazy-load your LCP element; lazy-load everything below the fold.
  • Delete unused third-party scripts before optimising the ones you keep.
  • Reserve space for every image, embed, and dynamic banner to kill layout shift.
  • Don't ignore server response time — no amount of front-end tuning fixes a slow backend.
